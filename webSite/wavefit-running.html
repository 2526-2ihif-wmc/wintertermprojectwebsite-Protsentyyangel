Benefits of Running

  • Improves Heart Health: Strengthens your heart and circulation
  • Burns Calories: Great for weight loss
  • Builds Endurance: Increases stamina
  • Mental Health: Reduces stress and improves mood
  • Strengthens Bones: Builds bone density
  • Better Sleep: Helps you sleep deeper

Types of Running

Easy Run

Comfortable pace where you can talk. Most runs should be easy.

Tempo Run

Comfortably hard pace. You can speak short sentences but not full conversations.

Interval Training

Alternate between fast and slow. Example: 5x (3min fast, 2min slow).

Long Run

Longer distance at easy pace. Builds endurance. Once per week.

Type How Often Benefits
Easy Run 3-4 times/week Builds base fitness
Tempo Run 1 time/week Improves speed
Intervals 1 time/week Increases speed
Long Run 1 time/week Builds endurance

Proper Running Form

  • Posture: Body upright with slight forward lean
  • Head: Look ahead, not down
  • Shoulders: Relaxed and down
  • Arms: Bent at 90 degrees, swing naturally
  • Hands: Relaxed, not tight fists
  • Feet: Land on midfoot, not heels
  • Steps: Quick and light, 170-180 per minute

Mistakes to Avoid

  • Running Too Fast: Most beginners run easy runs too fast
  • Long Steps: Don't overstride
  • No Warm-up: Always walk or jog slowly for 5-10 minutes first
  • Ignoring Pain: Stop if you feel sharp pain
  • Wrong Shoes: Get proper running shoes
  • Too Much Too Soon: Don't increase distance more than 10% per week

Nutrition for Runners

Proper nutrition is crucial for running performance, recovery, and preventing injuries. Runners have unique nutritional needs depending on their training volume and goals.

Pre-Run Nutrition (30-60 minutes before)

  • Short runs (under 1 hour): Light snack - banana, granola bar, or toast
  • Long runs (1-2 hours): Carbs + protein - oatmeal with banana or toast with peanut butter
  • Timing: Eat 30-60 minutes before to avoid cramping
  • Hydration: Drink water 2-3 hours and then 15 minutes before

During-Run Nutrition (for runs over 90 minutes)

  • Carbs: 30-60g per hour from sports drinks, gels, or energy bars
  • Electrolytes: Sodium helps with hydration and muscle cramps
  • Hydration: 400-800ml water per hour depending on conditions
  • Testing: Practice during training, not on race day

Post-Run Recovery Nutrition (within 30 minutes)

  • Carbs + Protein: 3-4:1 ratio for optimal recovery
  • Examples: Chocolate milk, protein smoothie with fruit, yogurt with granola
  • Timing: Critical within 30-60 minutes of finishing
  • Full meal: Eat a balanced meal within 2-3 hours

Daily Nutrition for Runners

  • Carbs: 5-7g per kg for moderate training, 7-10g+ for high volume
  • Protein: 1.4-1.6g per kg daily (slightly higher than non-runners)
  • Fats: 20-25% of total calories for hormone production
  • Iron: Important for oxygen transport (especially for female runners)
  • Antioxidants: Berries, leafy greens to reduce inflammation

Common Running Injuries & Prevention

Running injuries are common but preventable with proper training, recovery, and technique. Early intervention is key to avoiding long-term problems.

Most Common Running Injuries

  • Runner's Knee: Pain around the kneecap - prevent with strength training and proper form
  • Plantar Fasciitis: Heel pain - stretch calves regularly and use proper footwear
  • IT Band Syndrome: Knee/hip pain - foam roll and strengthen glutes
  • Shin Splints: Front of shin pain - increase gradually, avoid hard surfaces
  • Stress Fractures: Severe bone pain - usually from overtraining without recovery

Injury Prevention Strategies

  • Progressive Overload: Increase weekly mileage by no more than 10% per week
  • Cross Training: 2-3x per week of swimming, cycling, or strength training
  • Strength Training: Focus on glutes, hamstrings, core (2-3x per week)
  • Flexibility Work: Dynamic stretching before runs, static after (10-15 minutes)
  • Proper Footwear: Get gait analyzed and choose appropriate shoes
  • Adequate Recovery: Include easy runs, rest days, and proper sleep (7-9 hours)
  • Warm-Up & Cool-Down: 5-10 minutes each for every run

What to Do If Injured

  • Rest: Stop running immediately if sharp pain occurs
  • Ice: 15-20 minutes after activity
  • Compression & Elevation: Reduce swelling
  • Seek Medical Help: See a sports medicine doctor if pain persists
  • Cross Training: Stay active with swimming or cycling while recovering
  • Gradual Return: Start with walk-run intervals, increase slowly

Recovery Strategies Between Runs

  • Easy Runs: 60-70% max heart rate for active recovery
  • Sleep: 7-9 hours per night is crucial for adaptation
  • Foam Rolling: 10 minutes daily targets tight muscles
  • Stretching: Especially calves, hamstrings, hip flexors
  • Ice Baths: Optional, but may help reduce inflammation after long runs
